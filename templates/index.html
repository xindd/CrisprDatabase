To face the selective pressures imposed by viruses, prokaryotes and eukaryotes evolve genetic silencing systems to fight against viruses, named clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeat (CRISPR)-Cas and RNA interference (RNAi), respectively. While the CRISPR–Cas modules are adaptive immune systems present in most archaea and many bacteria to provide sequence-specific protection against foreign DNA or RNA, RNAi is cellular gene silencing of sequence specific degradation of target mRNA by complementary short interfering RNAs (siRNAs) in eukaryotes. Meanwhile, viruses also evolve the corresponding anti-proteins (anti-CRISPRs, Acrs) and viral suppressors of RNA silencing (VSRs) to inhibit these two defense systems respectively. 

SRGIdb:

An online database of suppressors of CRISPR-Cas and RNAi mediated defense systems for easy browsing of published anti-CRISPRs (Acrs) and viral suppressors of RNA silencing (VSRs).

Viral suppressors of RNA silencing (VSR):

To counteract host RNAi antiviral defense, viruses from almost all plant virus genera (and also from some insect and mammal viruses) encode viral suppressors of RNA silencing (VSRs) which inhibit key steps of cellular RNAi system.

Anti-CRISPRs (ACR):

Since CRISPR-Cas systems provide a potent defense mechanism against phage infection. On the contrary, phages and other mobile genetic elements possess anti-CRISPR proteins to inhibit these systems.
